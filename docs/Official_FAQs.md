## Eligibility & Teams

**Q: Can team members be from different universities?**

A: No, all team members must be enrolled at the same university.

**Q: Can a university have more than one team?**

A: Ye s , registrations will take place on a first-come, first-served basis with a single
university being able to register as many teams as possible until all slots are filled.

**Q: Can graduate and undergraduate students be on the same team?**

A: Yes, teams may include both graduate and undergraduate students.

**Q: Is there an age limit or minimum year requirement?**

A: There is no age limit or minimum year requirement. All currently enrolled bachelor and
master university students are eligible to participate.

**Q: Can I be on multiple teams?**

A: No, each participant may only be a member of one team.

**Q: Are PhD students allowed to compete?**

A: No, those that are currently enrolled or have completed a PhD are not allowed to
compete.

**Q: What if I'm a CFA Level I/II/III candidate — am I still eligible?**

A: Yes, CFA candidates are eligible to participate. Only current CFA charterholders are
ineligible to compete.

**Q: What if my university doesn't have a finance or CS program — can I still participate?**

A: Yes, students from any academic program may participate. The competition welcomes
diverse perspectives and interdisciplinary teams.

**Q: What if a team member drops out?**

A: Team members can be replaced up to one week before the competition. However, teams
must maintain at least three members to remain eligible.

page instructions for details. Registration closes February 20th.

A: See the timeline below:

Contact details are provided in the Official Rules.

**Q: Will there be virtual/remote participation options, or is everything in-person?**

## Stage 1 – Concept Submission

**Q: Do I need working code for Stage 1?**

A: No. Stage 1 is a concept submission only. You will build the working prototype in Stage 2.

Q **: What makes a strong concept submission?**

A: A clear problem definition, a feasible solution approach, demonstrated understanding of
investment industry impact, and thoughtful ethical considerations.

**Q: What format should the 2–4 page submission be in?**

A: Submissions should be in Word (.docx) or PDF format. Use the submission template
provided in Appendix A.


**Q: Is there a word count limit or specific formatting requirements?**

A: Submissions should be 2–4 pages. Use the provided template in Appendix A for guidance
on structure and content expectations.

**Q: Can we include diagrams, flowcharts, or mock-ups in the concept submission?**

A: Yes, diagrams and flowcharts are encouraged to illustrate your solution. These count
toward the 2–4 page limit.

## Stage 2 – Prototype Development......................................................................

**Q: What programming languages can I use?**

A: Common languages such as Python, R, JavaScript/Typescript are acceptable. If you plan
to use a different language, contact the host for approval.

**Q: Can we use proprietary/closed-source AI APIs (like OpenAI, Anthropic)?**

A: Yes, you may use closed-source APIs from OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google. Document
what you use and explain why it fits your solution.

**Q: Can I use open-source libraries?**

A: Yes, with proper attribution. See Rule 1.2 on plagiarism and Rule 1.3 on intellectual
property rights in the Official Rules.

**Q: What counts as "verifiable, working code"?**

A: Your solution must produce functional, testable output — not a conceptual demo or
mock-up. Partially completed code is acceptable if there is a clear path to completion.

**Q: Do we need to deploy our solution, or is a GitHub repo sufficient?**

A: For tools or web apps, the solution should be hosted via GitHub Pages or an equivalent
public deployment. You must provide links to both the front-end and the source code
repository. For non-web solutions, a GitHub repository with clear setup instructions is
sufficient.

**Q: What if our solution requires paid APIs or cloud compute — who covers costs?**

A: Teams are responsible for any costs associated with their solution. Google Gemini offers
a free tier with no credit card required. Anthropic and OpenAI have researcher and
university programs that your institution may already be involved in. We recommend
speaking with your university about API credits or cloud computing resources that may be
available to students.

**Q: What if our solution requires real-time data feeds we can't afford?**


A: All data must be publicly available to ensure reproducibility. If real-time feeds are cost-
prohibitive, consider using historical data, free data sources, or designing your solution to
work with delayed/sample data that demonstrates the concept.

**Q: Is there a specific timeline for Stage 2 development?**

A: Stage 2 runs from March 26 to April 9. You will have approximately two weeks to develop
your working solution with mentor support.

**Q: Does my prototype need a user interface?**

A: A user interface is not required, but your solution must produce interpretable outputs,
visualizations, or analytical results.

**Q: What format should the Stage 2 submission be in?**

A: Use the Stage 2 submission template provided in Appendix B, along with your code
repository and deployment links.

## Mentorship

**Q: What can my mentor help with?**

A: Mentors provide industry guidance, feedback on your approach, and help refine your
solution. They cannot write code for you or do your work.

**Q: How often will we meet with our mentor? What's the expected time commitment?**

A: Mentors will provide 2–3 hours of guidance over the 2-week Stage 2 development period.
Meeting frequency is flexible and arranged between your team and mentor based on your
needs.

**Q: How are mentors assigned to teams?**

A: Mentors are randomly assigned by the host.

**Q: Can we contact our mentor outside scheduled sessions?**

A: This is up to your mentor. Discuss communication preferences and availability when you
first connect.

## Stage 3 – Final Presentation

**Q: How long are the final presentations?**

A: Presentations are 10 minutes, followed by 5 minutes of Q&A with the judges.


**Q: Will presentations be in-person or virtual?**

A: Final presentations are in-person in late April. The top 5 teams will present live to a panel
of judges.

**Q: Who are the judges?**

A: Judges are industry experts, including CFA charterholders and investment professionals.
Specific judges will be announced closer to the final presentations.

**Q: Can we do a live demo, or is it slides only?**

A: Live demos are permitted, but keep in mind you only have 10 minutes and technical
difficulties will count against your time.

## AI Tools & Code

**Q: Can I use generative AI (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot) to help write code?**

A: Yes, but you must understand and be able to explain the code in your solution. You may
be asked to explain it in your presentation. Document any significant AI assistance used.

**Q: What counts as plagiarism for code?**

A: Using others' code without attribution. Open-source code with proper licensing and
citation is acceptable.

## Data & Technical

**Q: Can I use publicly available financial datasets?**

A: Yes, with proper citation and compliance with the dataset's terms of use.

**Q: Can I use proprietary data from my employer?**

A: No, all data must be open access to ensure reproducibility.

**Q: Are there any restricted data types we cannot use?**

A: You cannot use proprietary data from your employer or any non-public data. All data
must be publicly available to ensure reproducibility.

**Q: Can we use synthetic/simulated data if real data isn't available?**

A: Yes, synthetic or simulated data is acceptable. Document how the data was generated
and ensure your approach could work with real data.

**Q: What if our solution involves trading signals — are there compliance concerns?**


A: There are no compliance restrictions on developing trading signals. Just be aware that
winning solutions will be made public when showcased on RPC Labs, a public CFA Institute
GitHub repository.

**Q: Do we need to worry about model explainability requirements?**

A: While not strictly required, explainability strengthens your solution. Judges will evaluate
how well you can explain your model's outputs and decision-making process during the
presentation.

## Intellectual Property

**Q: Who owns the intellectual property for solutions developed during the competition?**

A: Teams retain full ownership of their solutions. By participating, teams grant CFA Institute
and the Host a non-exclusive license to showcase submissions on CFA Institute platforms
(such as public GitHub pages or competition dashboards) for educational and promotional
purposes. CFA Institute will not commercialize team solutions. All code must be released
under the MIT License.

**Q: What is the MIT License?**

A: The MIT License is a simple, permissive open-source license that allows anyone to use,
modify, and distribute your code freely while you retain copyright ownership. It's one of the
most widely used open-source licenses and ensures your solution can benefit the broader
investment community.

**Q: If I use MIT License, can a company just take my solution and commercialize it?**

A: The MIT License is the standard open-source license used in this competition. By
participating, you are open-sourcing your solution for the broader investment community's
benefit. The license allows anyone to use, modify, and distribute your code, but you retain
copyright ownership.

**Q: Can I continue developing my solution after the competition for commercial purposes?**

A: Yes, you retain full ownership of your solution. You can continue developing it for any
purpose, including commercial use.

## Recognition

**Q: Can I put this on my resume or LinkedIn?**

A: Yes. Winners will receive a digital certificate from CFA Institute.


## Responsible AI

**Q: What ethical considerations should I address in my submission?**

A: Consider how your solution handles: data privacy (is personal or sensitive data
involved?), bias (could your model produce unfair or skewed outputs?), transparency (can
users understand how decisions are made?), and reliability (what happens if the model is
wrong?). You don't need to solve every issue — but you should demonstrate awareness of
potential risks and explain how you plan to mitigate them.

**Q: Do I need to follow a specific responsible AI framework?**

A: No specific framework is required. Focus on identifying risks relevant to your solution —
such as data bias, lack of explainability, or potential for misuse — and explain how you plan
to address them. Your Stage 1 ethical considerations will be used as the baseline for
evaluating your final solution.
