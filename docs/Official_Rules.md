## RULE 1: INTRODUCTION AND GENERAL PROVISIONS

The AI Investment Challenge is a competition between teams comprising 3-4 pe ople , to solve
a real-world investment problem using AI, then present their findings to a panel of judges.
The competition is conducted by CFA Institute and in collaboration with CFA Society United
Kingdom, with support and cooperation from CFA Institute. No purchase is necessary to
participate.

This competition is hosted by CFA Institute and CFA Society United Kingdom (the “Host”).
The Host is solely responsible for the administration of the AI Challenge. No decision by the
Host regarding the administration of the competition shall conflict with these rules and in the
event of any conflict, these rules shall govern.

The Host is committed to creating a welcoming and safe environment for the AI Challenge
where fairness, opportunity, and trust exist, allowing teams to leverage the diverse talents
and perspectives of their participants. Participants in the AI Investment Challenge are
considered to be ambassadors of their university or employer and thus are expected to
behave accordingly.

### 1.1 Conflicts of Interest

All participants in the AI Investment Challenge, including the Host, teams, team members,
mentors, and judges have an obligation to avoid actual or potential conflicts of interest with
their participation. A conflict of interest is any matter that could reasonably be expected to
impair an individual’s independence and objectivity or interfere with an individual’s duties. A
conflict may be actual or perceived. As an example, a conflict of interest may exist when a
participant or a participant’s family member or close friend is involved in activities that affect
the participant’s ability to perform his or her role fairly and impartially, but conflicts of interest
can arise even without a personal relationship.

To avoid any actual or perceived conflicts of interest, each participant must disclose to the
Host in writing any potential conflicts. Judges must further disclose any close personal or
professional relationship with team members or faculty advisors. Potential conflicts of
interest must be disclosed promptly upon becoming aware of the potential conflict.

The Host will investigate any disclosures of actual or potential conflicts of interest. If a
conflict of interest is found to exist and cannot be reasonably and timely cured, the individual
with the conflict of interest will be disqualified from participation. If the Host determines that
it is necessary to protect the integrity of the competition, the Host may disqualify a team
from the competition if any team member or advisor is found to have a conflict of interest
that is incapable of being reasonably and timely cured. The decision of the Host regarding
the existence of a conflict of interest and its determination regarding the disqualification of
any individual or team are final and binding on all teams, team members, mentors, and
judges.


### 1.2 Plagiarism

Plagiarism is defined as copying or using in substantially the same form materials prepared
by others without acknowledging the source of the material or identifying the author and
publisher of such material. Teams may not copy analysis (i.e., plagiarize) from another source.

Teams also must not:

```
a. use excerpts from articles or reports prepared by others either verbatim or with only
slight changes in wording without acknowledgment,
b. cite specific quotations without naming the specific references,
```
Sourced information should be properly cited using a generally accepted citation system.
Generally accepted citation systems include, but are not limited to, the Chicago Manual of
Style, the Harvard referencing system, and MLA (Modern Language Association) style.

Team members found to have plagiarized may be disqualified. The team’s university may also
be ineligible to participate in the following year’s competition. The decision of the Host
regarding plagiarism is final.

### 1.3 Third-Party Intellectual Property Rights

Related to the concept of plagiarism, respect of third-party intellectual property rights is
essential to the AI Investment Challenge. While teams are free to include text, images,
graphics, or other logos in their presentations as permitted by these rules, it is the obligation
of each team to ensure that the use of any third-party materials complies with all applicable
copyright and trademark laws. Third-party images included in the presentation must be
appropriately licensed from the images’ owners. Where legally necessary, teams should
secure the permission of the owners of any trademarks or service marks incorporated into
their presentation. By participating in the AI Investment Challenge, each team represents and
warrants that its presentation does not infringe or violate the copyright, trademark, trade
secret or other intellectual property right of any third party anywhere in the world.

## RULE 3: COMPETITION LEVELS

### 3.1 Round 1 – Concept Submission

Teams’ concept submissions shall be scored by the judges using the scoring sheet set forth
in Appendix A. The team s with the highest ranking after all concepts have been evaluated
shall advance to the next round. The number of teams advancing to Round 2 will be
announced at the launch webinar. In the event of a tie, ties shall be broken in favor of the
team(s) with the highest score on the Relevance criterion. If a tie remains, the next tiebreaker
is the Impact score, followed by Feasibility, then Innovation. If the foregoing fails to resolve
all ties, all tied teams will advance to Round 2.


Concept submissions must include the following:

```
a. Problem Definition
b. Solution Overview
c. Planned Implementation
d. Impact Assessment
e. Ethical Considerations
```
### 3.2 Round 2 – Mentorship & Solution Development

Teams will build a verifiable, working AI solution based on the Round 1 concept. Each team
will be paired with a CFA Charterholder mentor to guide them through refining and
implementing their solution. Teams are expected to produce functional code, not a
conceptual demo or mock-up.

Solutions must include the following:

```
a. Functional Code containing data acquisition/preprocessing, AI/ML model
implementation, and outputs or analytical results
```
```
b. Technical Explanation (2–4 pages) addressing architectural design, workflow, data
sources, discussion of results, model interpretability and AI usage. Technical
explanation must contain a 4 bullet executive summary.
```
```
c. Round 2 Evaluation Process: Round 2 evaluation consists of two components:
```
```
I. Code Review by volunteer code reviewers (separate from Round 1 judges and
mentors) who will score submissions on Functionality, Clarity, and Path to
Completion (see Appendix B); and
II. Executive Summary Ranked-Choice Voting by the mentors, who will review
executive summaries and rank the 9 teams other than their own based on Real-
World Value (see Appendix B2). The top 5 teams based on combined scores will
advance to Round 3. In the event of a tie, the team with the higher normalized
mentor ranking score advances. If still tied, the team with more first-place rankings
from mentors advances. If still tied, mentors will deliberate and vote.
```
### 3.3 Round 3 – Final Presentation

In the final round, teams will present the working solution to a panel of industry experts.
Teams will be scored based on the rubric in Appendix C. In the event of a tie, judges will
deliberate and then vote.

## RULE 4: USE OF AI TOOLS

Th e AI Investment Challenge is an AI competition. Th e us e of AI tools is no t only permitted
bu t expected. This competition reflects the real world, wher e professionals leverage every
available too l to solv e problems. Wha t matters is resourcefulness, transparenc y, and the
ability to communicate your approach clearly an d ethically.

### 4.1 Guiding Principles

```
a. Transparency: All AI use must be clearly communicated. Teams must disclose which
AI tools were used, how they were used, and why.
b. Reproducibility: Judges must be able to verify and reproduce your results. Solutions
that rely on AI must be structured so that outputs can be validated.
c. Accessibility: AI tools used must be reasonably accessible. Proprietary enterprise
tools that judges cannot access to verify results are not permitted.
```
### 4.2 Categories of AI Use

**AI as a Development or Writing Assistant** – Using AI tools (such as GitHub Copilot,
ChatGPT, or Claude) to help write, debug, or refine code or explanations. This use is
permitted without restriction. The submitted functional code is the reproducible artifact.
Teams must disclose such use in their Technical Explanation and Round 1 concept
submissions.

**AI as a Component of the Solution** – When the solution itself makes API calls to AI models
at runtime (e.g., using GPT-5.2 to analyze documents as part of the tool’s functionality).
This use is permitted but subject to the reproducibility requirements in Section 4 .4.

### 4.3 Approved AI Tools as a Component of the Solution

The following AI tools are approved for use as components of submitted solutions:

```
a. Frontier Foundation Models (API-based): OpenAI (GPT-5.2, GPT-5.2-mini, etc.),
Anthropic (Opus 4.5), Google (Gemini)
b. Open-Source Models: Llama, Mistral, Qwen, and similar openly available models.
Self-hosting and fine-tuning are permitted.
```

**Not Permitted:** Proprietary enterprise AI tools (e.g., Bloomberg models, internal corporate
models) or any AI service that judges could not reasonably access to verify results.

### 4.4 Reproducibility Requirements

If your solution uses AI API calls at runtime to produce results, judges must be able to
reproduce and verify those results. The following requirements apply:

**If reproduction costs ≤ $20:** Document the AI model(s) used, version/date, and
approximate token cost in your Technical Explanation.

**If reproduction costs > $20:** You must cache the API responses in your repository so that
judges can verify outputs without incurring the cost. Include:

```
a. Cached API responses (JSON or text format)
b. Documentation of model name, version, and parameters (e.g., temperature)
c. A method to run the solution using cached data (e.g., a --use-cached flag)
```
_The $20 threshold refers to the cost of running the solution once to reproduce results, not
total development costs._

### 4.5 Disclosure Requirements

All teams must include an AI Use Statement in their Technical Explanation that addresses:

```
a. Which AI tools were used during development
b. Which AI tools are integrated into the solution (if any)
c. How and why each AI tool was used
d. Estimated API costs for reproduction (if applicable)
```
### 4.6 Data Requirements

To ensure judges can verify and reproduce your solution, the following data requirements
apply:

- **Source Data Must Be Publicly Accessible**
    All source data used in your solution must be publicly available. Proprietary data
    from employers, paid data terminals (e.g., Bloomberg), or other sources that judges
    cannot freely access is not permitted. Teams must document all data sources with
    URLs or clear instructions for how to obtain the data.
- **Data You Create Is Part of Your Solution**
    If your solution involves creating derived datasets—such as labeled data, annotated
    datasets, scraped and processed public information, or synthetic training data—this
    is considered part of your work product. You must include this data in your
    repository so judges can reproduce your results.

**Documentation**

Your Technical Explanation must include a Data Sources section that describes:

1. all source data and how to access it,
2. any derived or created datasets and how they were produced, and


3. preprocessing steps applied.

## Appendix B2 – Round 2 Executive Summary Ranked-Choice

## Voting

```
Overview
```
```
In Round 2, each mentor ranks all team’s (excluding their own assigned team) round 2
Executive Summaries based on an assessment of Real-World Value (a combined metric of
Relevance and Impact).
```
```
How Ranked-Choice Voting Works
```
1. Each mentor ranks all eligible teams from 1 (highest) to N (lowest), where N is the
    number of teams they can evaluate.
2. Mentors may not rank their own assigned team.
3. Rankings are converted to points: 1st place = N points, 2nd place = N-1 points, etc.
4. Points from all mentors are summed for each team.

```
Combining Scores
```
```
The final Round 2 score combines code review scores (Appendix B) with mentor rankings:
```
- Code Review Component: Code review score (normalized to 50 points)


- Mentor Ranking Component: Sum of mentor ranking points (normalized to 50
    points)
- Final Score = Code Review Component + Mentor Ranking Component (out of 100)

The top 5 teams by combined score advance to Round 3 (Finals).

**_Why this works:_** _Code review assesses technical execution. A fully functional, well-
documented prototype (100 points) makes advancement entirely dependent on mentor
assessment of Real-World Value. A partially complete prototype with exceptional Real-World
Value can still advance if mentors rank it highly enough to offset the code review gap._

### Worked Example

**Scenario:** 10 teams in Round 2, 10 mentors. Each mentor ranks the 9 teams other than their
own assigned team.

**Step 1: Convert Rankings to Points**

```
With 9 teams to rank, points are assigned as: 1st place = 9 pts, 2nd = 8 pts, 3rd = 7
pts ... 9th = 1 pt
Formula: Points = (N + 1) – Rank, where N = number of teams ranked
Example: If Mentor A ranks Team X as 2nd place → Points = (9 + 1) – 2 = 8 points
```
**Step 2: Sum Points Across All Mentors**

```
Team X receives rankings from 9 mentors (their assigned Mentor J cannot rank
them):
Mentor A Mentor B Mentor C Mentor D Mentor E Mentor F Mentor G Mentor H Mentor I
Rank: 1 Rank: 2 Rank: 1 Rank: 3 Rank: 2 Rank: 1 Rank: 2 Rank: 4 Rank: 1
9 pts 8 pts 9 pts 7 pts 8 pts 9 pts 8 pts 6 pts 9 pts
```
```
(Mentor J is Team X’s assigned mentor and cannot rank them)
Team X Total Mentor Points = 9 + 8 + 9 + 7 + 8 + 9 + 8 + 6 + 9 = 73 points
```
**Step 3: Normalize Scores**

```
Normalization ensures both components contribute equally (50 points each) to the final
score.
Mentor Score Normalization Formula:
Normalized Mentor Score = (Total Mentor Points ÷ Max Possible Points) × 50
Max possible = 9 mentors × 9 points (if ranked 1st by all) = 81 points
Team X: (73 ÷ 81) × 50 = 45.1 points
Code Review Normalization Formula:
Normalized Code Score = (Code Review Score ÷ 100) × 50
Team X scores 82/100 on code review: (82 ÷ 100) × 50 = 41.0 points
```

**Step 4: Calculate Final Score**

```
Final Round 2 Score = Normalized Mentor Score + Normalized Code Score
Team X: 45.1 + 41.0 = 86.1 out of 100
```
